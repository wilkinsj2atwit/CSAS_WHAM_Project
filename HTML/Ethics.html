Antikythera Shipwreck

The Antikythera shipwreck, discovered in 1900 off the coast of the Greek island Antikythera, is one of the most significant underwater archaeological finds. It contained a treasure trove of artifacts from the ancient world, offering a glimpse into Greek technology, art, and daily life.

Antikythera Mechanism

Known as the world's first analog computer, this device was used to predict astronomical events and eclipses. Its intricate gears demonstrate the advanced engineering of ancient Greece.

Bronze Statue of Youth

A stunning representation of Greek sculpture, this statue depicts a young athlete or deity, showcasing the artistry of bronze casting in the Hellenistic period.

Glassware and Pottery

Beautifully preserved glassware and ceramics found in the wreck provide insight into ancient trade, daily life, and craftsmanship.

The Parthenon Marbles

The Parthenon Marbles, removed by Lord Elgin in the early 19th century, are currently housed in the British Museum. The Greek government has long requested their return, citing cultural heritage and ownership rights. The British Museum argues their preservation and accessibility are best served in London.
